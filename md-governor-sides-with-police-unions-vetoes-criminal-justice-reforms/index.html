MD Governor Sides With Police Unions, Vetoes Criminal Justice Reforms

News

Written by: 0x000216

May 19, 2018

Claire Bernish
May 25, 2015

(ANTIMEDIA) After recent unrest following the death of Freddie Gray, Maryland might be one of the first states brought to mind when discussing the need for criminal justice reform. Gov Larry Hogan seems to disagree. In a press release late Friday, the governor vetoed six pieces of legislation, including measures to refashion exploitative and contentious criminal laws.

In 2014, Maryland decriminalized marijuana, making its use and possession up to ten grams a simple civil offense with a fine of up to $100 for the first offense, up to $250 for a second, and up to $500 for each subsequent infraction.

In line with this relatively new legislation, SB 517 would have made smoking marijuana in public a civil offense carrying a fine of up to $500 and would have eliminated any criminal penalty for paraphernalia; but the governor didn’t feel these measures would outweigh the risks. In his veto, he cited “legal uncertainties” created around the consumption of the plant while driving, calling that “a serious threat to public safety. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration states: ‘While alcohol is clearly the predominant drug in fatal crashes, marijuana is the next drug most frequently found in crash-involved drivers . . . Marijuana, even in low to moderate doses, negatively affects driving performance in real situations.’”

Decriminalization serves several purposes, not the least of which is lessening the numbers of people behind bars for nonviolent offenses. Refusing to include possession of paraphernalia in that umbrella, therefore, seems contradictory to the intent of the law.

Another vetoed piece of legislation, SB 528, outlined greater restrictions for asset and property seizure and forfeiture — by any estimation an important stopgap to prevent law enforcement from overstepping its bounds. Setting a $300 minimum for the seizure of money, requiring notice to the owner whose property had been seized, and requiring conditions that either the target’s consent or that the target be actually charged with a federal criminal violation before seized property could be transferred to the government, the bill appeared to set reasonable limits with respect to individual rights. The governor apparently didn’t agree.

Noteworthy in his explanations for turning down what seemed to be decent reform measures, the governor stated he was complying with requests from the Maryland Chiefs of Police Association and the Maryland Sheriffs’ Association to veto. What this essentially indicates, is law enforcement resistance to reform and the governor’s capitulation at the expense of his constituents’ interests. How effective is a promise to change when the opportunity to change isn’t taken?

Without significant criminal justice reform, which seems highly unlikely in light of this latest resistance, another Baltimore-style uprising remains a likely possibility.
