Former Lee County Sheriff Urges Senator Rick Scott To Back Off Support For Criminal Sheriff He Appointed

Former Lee County Sheriff Rodney Shoap has not only taken the task up to write to Florida Governor Ron DeSantis about the questionable means that appointed Lee County Sheriff Carmine Marceno was able to fraudulently obtain certification in the state in violation of the law to be a police officer, but he has also written to the Florida Commission on Ethics concerning the complaint filed by Deanna Williams against Marceno concerning his unethical pursuit of her as a victim of crime and subsequent impregnation and demands for her to murder their unborn child.  Shoap has pointed out the lack of investigation by the Florida Department of Investigation (FDLE) based on Ms. Williams’ complaint, as well as FDLE’s response to it.  Now, the former sheriff has written to US Senator Rick Scott, the man that appointed Marceno as sheriff, urging him to pull back his support of Marceno due to his criminal and unethical activity.

In a letter dated September 12, 2019, supplied to SonsOfLibertyMedia.com, Shoap addressed Senator Scott stating that because Scott had appointed Marceno as sheriff at the request of former Sheriff Mike Scott, that he, in his official capacity as Senator, “may position yourself in support of Sheriff Marceno’s campaign to remain as Lee County Sheriff.”

Sheriff Shoap urged Scott to “give this careful consideration before lending support to this candidate.”

Specifically, Shoap cites the information surrounding Ms. Williams’ claim.

He writes the following:

Sheriff Marceno, while serving as the Undersheriff, and in his official capacity, established a personal relationship with Ms Deanna Williams who had reported a theft of funds to the Lee County Sheriff’s Office (LCSO). An active investigation was started by a detective in the Economic Crimes Unit on behalf of Ms. Williams’ complaint. Marceno interfered with the investigation being conducted by a LCSO detective and used his influence as Undersheriff to form a relationship with Ms. Williams that was sexual in nature. Marceno made the preliminary contact with the victim, Williams. Marceno had not received any request from the victim or any other persons engaged in the case investigation; 

  • Williams said in her statement to FDLE criminal investigators that she resisted Marceno’s forward attempts but finally gave in to him hoping he would help her with her case (a loss of $200,000) as he promised;
    • FDLE criminal investigators listened to Ms. Williams statement but never looked at and didn’t make copies of the text messages and emails because they determined, through her story, that the events had no criminal predicate. None the less, Ms. Williams has copies of Sheriff Marceno’s intimate conversations with her.
    • While dating, Ms. Williams said that since Marceno enjoyed having sexual intercourse out in public, they routinely engaged in intercourse inside his Lee County Sheriff’s Office assigned unmarked patrol car and that pictures may have been taken by him while they were in the act of sexual intercourse;
    • As a result, Ms. Williams became pregnant and when notified of the pregnancy Marceno forcefully requested she abort the child;
    • In addition, Ms. Williams detailed how Marceno took her to his house to show her his renovations but they had sexual intercourse there and she believes he either took photographs or videos without her knowledge at the time; 
    • Text messages and emails between Marceno and Williams exist detailing these allegations; and,
  • Williams decided to take Sheriff Marceno to court and filed a paternity case. During at least one of the Hearings Marceno made the statement: (paraphrased) ‘if it is my baby I will take care of it but I want custody’.

The second part of Shoap’s letter deals with the crimes of impersonating a police officer and fraud committed by Marceno.

“A second issue involves Sheriff Marceno’s actual police certification,” Shoap writes.  “It would appear based on his background that he did not qualify to take the police certification exam. The requirements for taking the police certification exam are two: First, the candidate must have graduated from a Florida POST qualifying police academy, and second, the candidate must have served in full time capacity as a sworn officer for 12 full months.”

Shoap continued, “When reviewing Sheriff Marceno’s background, he did graduate from a Florida POST approved police academy. However, Sheriff Marceno has never served for 12 full months as a sworn police officer anywhere.  A FDLE form was submitted so that Marceno could take the exam and it lists Marceno as employed full time as a Suffolk County, New York Park Ranger. And Marceno may have served as a Park Ranger but according to all of his time sheets he served as a Part Time Seasonal Ranger, nothing more. That negates Sheriff Marceno’s qualifications to take the certification exam and puts in jeopardy any arrest(s) he has made or supervised as well as any training he has conducted. All could be vacated once challenged in any court of law.  The FDLE form was simply in error.”

Sheriff Shoap provide the following two documents to Senator Scott as evidence.

“In addition, there is an issue involving Sheriff Marceno’s applications for employment,” Shoap added.  “When comparing his Naples Police Department, Collier County Sheriff’s Office and Lee County Sheriff’s Office applications, his past employers along with dates of employment do not match and some areas are not complete such as the question: were you full time or part time being left blank. That question coincides with the FDLE form that listed him as full time when he was not.”

“These application issues, if substantiated, could be criminal and civil in nature,” he concluded.  “Further, these applications are under oath and require the applicant to be truthful or face criminal penalties.”

Finally, I’ll add that Marceno’s own hand penned that he was “part time” and not full time while working in Suffolk County, New York.
